Part III. Storage and Execution

Chapter 11. Transactions

Chapter 11 Summary: What We Learned

Page Topic Takeaway in One Sentence
101 Why transactions exist Without transactions, concurrent writes and crashes leave data in inconsistent states — transactions make correctness automatic.
102 The meaning of atomicity All-or-nothing: either every operation in a transaction happens, or none do — no partial updates survive.
103 Consistency in practice The database enforces constraints (PK, FK, CHECK) at commit time; application-level invariants are your responsibility.
104 Isolation and interference Isolation prevents one transaction's intermediate state from being visible to another — the I in ACID is the hardest to get right.
105 Durability and persistence Once a transaction commits, its effects survive crashes and power loss — guaranteed by the WAL.
106 Transaction boundaries Group logically related reads and writes into one transaction; keep transactions short to reduce lock contention.
107 Commit and rollback COMMIT makes changes permanent; ROLLBACK undoes all changes since the transaction began.
108 Multi-step updates Money transfers, order placements, inventory decrements — all require multiple writes that must be atomic.
109 Real examples of broken logic Dirty reads, lost updates, and phantom reads cause real-world bugs: double-spending, inconsistent reports, corrupted data.
Reveal: How do these ideas connect to the next chapter?
Chapter 11 introduced transactions and ACID — the contract that keeps your data correct. But the I in ACID (Isolation) is the hardest letter to get right, and it is the bridge to Chapter 12. You will learn how databases handle concurrent transactions: locks, MVCC, isolation levels, and the anomalies that occur when isolation is too weak. If Chapter 11 was about why transactions exist, Chapter 12 is about how they coexist without stepping on each other.
