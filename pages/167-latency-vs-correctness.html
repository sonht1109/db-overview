Part IV. Distributed Systems

Chapter 17. Consistency in Distributed Databases

Latency vs correctness

Every distributed database forces you to make a choice that has no perfect answer: how fast do you want to be, and how wrong are you willing to be? These two goals pull in opposite directions. Stronger correctness requires more coordination between nodes — and coordination costs time. This chapter puts a name and a framework on that tension.

What "correctness" means here

In the context of distributed databases, correctness is about what a reader sees relative to what a writer has stored. The gold standard is linearizability: every operation appears to take effect instantaneously at a single moment in time, and every subsequent read reflects every prior write. It behaves as if the entire distributed system were one perfectly reliable single-node database.

Achieving linearizability across nodes requires a coordination round before a read or write can complete: nodes must agree on ordering. That round-trip — even at the speed of light — takes time. A client in Tokyo reading from a leader in Frankfurt waits at least ~120 ms for one coordination round-trip, no matter how fast the hardware is.

Note: This is not an engineering limitation you can optimize away. The speed of light, geographic distance, and the laws of physics set a hard floor on coordination latency. It is a fundamental constraint, not a bug.

The spectrum: from fast-but-stale to slow-but-safe

Consistency is not binary. Real systems expose a dial with several named positions. Important: the models below address two different dimensions. Linearizability is about recency — what order reads and writes appear in relative to real time. Serializability is about isolation — whether concurrent transactions produce results equivalent to some serial execution. They are orthogonal: a system can be serializable but not linearizable (e.g., snapshot isolation), or linearizable but not serializable (e.g., linearizable key-value store). The table lists them together for comparison, not because they are points on a single axis.

Level What the reader sees Typical extra latency Example
Linearizable Latest write, globally ordered High (coordination round) etcd, Zookeeper, Spanner
Serializability Transactions appear in some serial order Medium–high CockroachDB, Postgres with SSI
Read-your-writes Your own writes are visible to you Low–medium Sticky sessions, read-after-write routing
Monotonic reads You never see data go backwards in time Low Session-level replica pinning
Eventual Replica may be arbitrarily behind Near-zero Cassandra default, DynamoDB default reads

Moving down the table: reads get faster and more available, but the gap between "what a reader sees" and "what is actually stored" widens.

Why the tradeoff is unavoidable: a quick proof

Suppose you want a read to always return the most recent write. The reader must somehow know what the most recent write is. In a distributed system, that knowledge requires communicating with other nodes. Communication takes time. Therefore: strong correctness implies latency. This is not a matter of clever engineering — it is a consequence of the network partition and message-delay models that underpin all distributed systems.

The PACELC model (an extension of CAP) makes this explicit: even when the system is not partitioned, there is a tradeoff between latency and consistency (the "ELC" part: Else, Latency vs Consistency).

Choosing your point on the dial

The right choice depends on the cost asymmetry for your specific workload:

  • High cost of staleness (bank balance, inventory that can go negative, authentication tokens) → pay the latency for linearizability or at least read-your-writes.
  • Low cost of staleness (view counts, search index freshness, analytics dashboards) → accept eventual consistency and enjoy lower latency and higher availability.
  • Mixed workloads → most production systems use tunable consistency per operation. DynamoDB lets you request strongly-consistent reads for critical paths while defaulting to eventual reads elsewhere.

Key takeaways

  • Coordination costs time. Every guarantee stronger than eventual consistency requires at least one synchronous round-trip between nodes.
  • The PACELC model captures the permanent latency/consistency tradeoff that exists even without network partitions.
  • Staleness has a price too. Choosing low latency means accepting the risk of serving outdated data — and that risk must be weighed against its business cost.
  • Most real systems make the dial tunable per query or operation, rather than committing the whole database to one point on the spectrum.
