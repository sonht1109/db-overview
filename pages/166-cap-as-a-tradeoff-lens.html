Part IV. Distributed Systems

Chapter 17. Consistency in Distributed Databases

CAP as a tradeoff lens

The CAP theorem is one of the most cited — and most misunderstood — ideas in distributed systems. State it plainly: a distributed system cannot simultaneously guarantee all three of Consistency, Availability, and Partition tolerance. When a network partition occurs, it must sacrifice one. That's the theorem. But CAP's real value isn't the proof — it's as a thinking tool that forces you to ask the right questions when designing or choosing a distributed database.

The Three Properties, Precisely

Before using CAP as a lens, you need the exact definitions, because informal shorthand leads to bad decisions.

Property What it means
C — Consistency Every read returns the most recent write (or an error). All nodes see the same data at the same time. This is linearizability, not the "C" in ACID.
A — Availability Every request to a non-failed node receives a response — not an error, not a timeout. The response may not reflect the very latest write.
P — Partition tolerance The system continues operating when network partitions split nodes into groups that cannot communicate.

Note: "Partition tolerance" is not really optional. Real networks drop packets, switches reboot, and cables get cut. Designing as if partitions cannot happen is designing for a world that doesn't exist. So in practice, the real choice is: when a partition strikes, do you sacrifice C or A?

The Forced Choice During a Partition

Picture two data center regions, East and West, both serving your application. A backbone failure severs the link between them. Requests are still arriving at both sides. What does each region do?

CP systems (Consistent + Partition-tolerant) refuse to answer rather than answer wrongly. HBase, Zookeeper, and etcd work this way. A client might get an error or block waiting for the partition to heal. The data you do get is guaranteed to be current.

AP systems (Available + Partition-tolerant) keep answering, even if the answer might be stale. Cassandra and CouchDB lean here. A client always gets a response; after the partition heals, the system reconciles conflicting writes. The price is that two clients in different regions can briefly see different truths.

CAP as a Lens, Not a Binary Switch

The danger in treating CAP as a strict "pick two" menu is that it suggests a one-time architectural decision. Reality is more nuanced:

  • Granularity matters. A single system can make different tradeoffs per operation. DynamoDB lets you choose strongly consistent or eventually consistent reads per request.
  • Partitions are rare but not zero. Most of the time there is no partition, and the CP/AP distinction is invisible. The system's behavior under normal conditions (latency, throughput, staleness) often matters more day to day.
  • The "C" in CAP is strict. Linearizability is a strong guarantee. Many systems offer weaker consistency (causal, session, monotonic read) that sits between the two extremes — enough consistency for many applications, with less availability cost.

Note: CAP was formalized by Eric Brewer in 2000 and proved by Gilbert and Lynch in 2002. Brewer himself later acknowledged it oversimplifies: the PACELC model extends CAP by also considering the latency vs. consistency tradeoff even when no partition exists — because in practice, replicating synchronously costs latency whether or not the network is split.

Applying the Lens: Which Tradeoff Fits Your Application?

Application type Partition behavior you likely want Why
Bank account balance CP — refuse the request A stale balance can cause overdrafts
Shopping cart contents AP — serve stale, merge later A missing item for a moment is acceptable
Leader election (Zookeeper) CP — must have one leader Two leaders = split-brain disaster
DNS record lookup AP — serve cached answer Stale IP for seconds is fine; unavailability is not
Inventory "in stock" flag Depends — often AP with compensation Small oversell risk beats cart errors at checkout

The right question when evaluating a distributed database isn't "is this CP or AP?" It's: what happens to my users and my data when a partition strikes? If incorrect data causes harm (financial, safety, legal), lean CP. If unavailability causes harm (lost revenue, poor UX), lean AP.

Reveal: Why can't we just fix the network and always have all three?
The CAP proof shows the conflict is fundamental, not an engineering limitation. During a partition, any message between the two sides is lost — by definition. So no amount of clever engineering can let West instantly learn about East's write. West must either wait (losing availability) or proceed with what it knows (losing consistency). Faster networks shrink the window of inconsistency in AP systems, but they cannot eliminate the logical impossibility during an active partition.
