Part VI. Engineering Practice

Chapter 24. Designing for Reliability

Chapter 24 Summary: What We Learned

Chapter Contents

Page Topic Takeaway in One Sentence
231 Backups and restores Regular backups (full + incremental) with tested restore procedures — untested backups are not backups at all.
232 Point-in-time recovery WAL archiving lets you restore to any moment, not just the last backup — critical for recovering from logical errors.
233 High availability basics Redundancy (replicas), automatic failover, and health checks keep the database available when individual components fail.
234 Failure testing Chaos engineering: deliberately kill nodes, networks, and disks to verify your recovery procedures work under real conditions.
235 Schema migrations in production Use expand-contract pattern: add columns, dual-write, migrate data, then remove old columns. Never rename; always add and deprecate.
236 Monitoring and alerts Track key metrics: query latency, error rates, replication lag, disk usage, connection count. Alert on trends, not thresholds.
237 Capacity planning Project growth in data volume, query rate, and connection count; plan upgrades before you hit limits.
238 Operational runbooks Documented step-by-step procedures for common incidents: failover, restore, scaling, and troubleshooting.
239 Common failure stories Running out of disk during a long weekend, replication lag causing data inconsistency, misconfigured connection pools — learn from others' pain.
