Part VI. Engineering Practice

Chapter 26. Choosing a Database

Managed vs self-hosted

Once you have decided which database to use, a second decision immediately follows: run it yourself, or let a cloud provider manage it for you? This choice shapes your operational burden, your cost structure, your hiring profile, and your ability to move quickly when something goes wrong. Neither answer is universally correct, but the trade-offs are well understood and the decision can be made with a short checklist.

What "Managed" Actually Means

A managed database service (Amazon RDS, Google Cloud SQL, MongoDB Atlas, PlanetScale, Supabase) takes ownership of a specific set of operational concerns:

  • Provisioning and configuration — spinning up instances, setting memory and CPU, configuring storage.
  • Automated backups — point-in-time recovery with defined retention windows.
  • Patch management — the vendor applies database engine updates (with configurable maintenance windows).
  • Failover — automated primary/replica promotion on hardware failure.
  • Monitoring basics — CPU, memory, disk, connections; often integrated with the cloud provider's dashboard.

What managed services typically do not handle: schema design, query optimisation, index strategy, application-level connection pooling, and cost control when queries are inefficient.

Important: Managed does not mean maintenance-free. A slow query on a managed RDS instance is your problem to diagnose and fix, not Amazon's.

What "Self-Hosted" Actually Means

Self-hosted means you run the database process on infrastructure you control — whether on-premise servers, cloud VMs (EC2, GCE), or containers in Kubernetes. You are responsible for everything the managed service would otherwise provide:

  • Installation, version management, and upgrades
  • Backup scripts, restore testing, and offsite storage
  • Replication setup and failover automation
  • Monitoring, alerting, and on-call runbooks
  • Security patches and OS hardening

This is not inherently expensive or slow — large, experienced teams run self-hosted PostgreSQL clusters at enormous scale. But it requires expertise, process, and time.

The Cost Trade-Off

The most common objection to managed services is cost. Cloud-managed databases carry a premium — typically 30–60% above the raw compute and storage cost of a self-hosted equivalent. For a small team this premium is almost always worth it. For a large team operating hundreds of database instances, the math sometimes shifts.

The key comparison is total cost of ownership, not sticker price:

Self-hosted true cost =
  infrastructure cost
  + (DBA/ops engineer hours × blended rate)
  + opportunity cost of engineer time not on product
  + incident response cost (downtime × revenue impact)
  + cost of backup infrastructure and testing

A $500/month managed RDS instance versus a $250/month EC2 instance running PostgreSQL is not a $250/month saving if a DBA spends 10 hours/month on the self-hosted instance — that's $1,000–1,500 in engineering time at typical rates.

When Self-Hosted Wins

Self-hosting makes sense in specific situations:

  1. Regulatory or data-sovereignty requirements prevent data from leaving a specific physical location or cloud region. A managed service in the right region may still satisfy this, but on-premise self-hosting is sometimes the only compliant option.
  2. Massive scale where the volume discount from operating your own infrastructure outweighs the operational cost. Companies at Netflix or Uber scale operate their own database infrastructure for precisely this reason.
  3. Configuration control unavailable in managed offerings — specific OS tuning, custom extensions, non-standard replication topologies, or features not yet available in the managed version.
  4. Database version control — managed services sometimes lag behind the latest engine version. If your application requires a bleeding-edge feature, you may need to self-host.

Key Takeaways

  • Managed services transfer infrastructure operations to the vendor; application-layer concerns (schema, query performance) always remain yours.
  • The true cost of self-hosting includes engineering time, not just infrastructure; at small scale, managed is often cheaper in total.
  • Self-hosting is justified by regulatory requirements, massive scale, or configuration needs that managed offerings cannot satisfy.
  • Evaluate on total cost of ownership, not monthly infrastructure sticker price alone.
