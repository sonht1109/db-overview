Part III. Storage and Execution

Chapter 9. Pages, Records, and Files

Chapter 9 Summary: What We Learned

Chapter Contents

Page Topic Takeaway in One Sentence
081 Why databases use pages Pages (usually 4–16 KB) are the unit of I/O — reading/writing whole pages amortizes disk seek costs and aligns with OS and hardware.
082 Heap files and page layouts A heap file is an unordered collection of pages; rows are stored wherever there is space, identified by page + slot.
083 Row stores and column stores Row stores optimize for transactional workloads (read/write full rows); column stores optimize for analytics (scan few columns).
084 Fixed-length and variable-length records Fixed-length records are predictable and fast; variable-length records (TEXT, BLOB) need extra bookkeeping for offsets.
085 Slotted pages A slotted page has a header with slot array pointing to variable-length records — records can move within the page without changing their ID.
086 Free space management The engine tracks free space per page so INSERTs know where to go; fragmented free space wastes I/O.
087 Record identifiers A record ID (page number + slot number) gives O(1) access to any row — the basis of secondary index lookups.
088 Updates and fragmentation In-place updates can overflow a page; some engines use forwarding pointers or MVCC to avoid moving referenced rows.
089 How rows are found on disk Without an index: sequential scan. With a B-tree index: traverse root to leaf, read record ID, fetch the page and slot.
