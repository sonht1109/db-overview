Chapter 21. Graph Databases

Chapter 21 Summary: What We Learned

You have completed all 9 pages of Chapter 21: Graph Databases. This summary page pulls together the main ideas from each topic so you can quickly review what you learned, identify anything that still feels fuzzy, and confirm you are ready to move on.

Chapter Contents

Page Topic Takeaway in One Sentence
201 Nodes, edges, and properties Graph databases model entities as nodes, relationships as edges, and attributes as properties on both.
202 Traversals and path queries Graph queries follow edges (JOINs in SQL) — multi-hop traversals are O(edges followed), not exponential as in relational JOINs.
203 Modeling relationships directly Relationships are first-class citizens, not foreign-key columns — they carry their own properties (weight, type, timestamp).
204 Graph query languages Cypher, Gremlin, SPARQL — graph-specific languages that express patterns of nodes and edges as visual ASCII art.
205 When joins become graph walks In SQL, a 5-hop relationship query requires 5 JOINs (and gets exponentially harder). In a graph DB, it is a single traversal.
206 Performance characteristics Index-free adjacency: each node stores direct pointers to its neighbors — traversal cost depends on edges traversed, not total data size.
207 Strengths of graph storage Recursive queries, path finding, pattern matching, and recommendation engines are natural in a graph model.
208 Limits and tradeoffs Graph databases are not general-purpose: poor at aggregation, full-table scans, and write-heavy workloads that are better suited to relational.
209 Good use cases Business intelligence dashboards, periodic reports, machine learning feature engineering, log analysis, financial reconciliation.
