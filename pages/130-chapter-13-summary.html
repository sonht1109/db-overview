Chapter Contents

Page Topic Takeaway in One Sentence
121 What can go wrong Power loss, OS crash, disk failure, or a transaction abort mid-write can leave data corrupt — recovery handles all of these.
122 The write-ahead log WAL is an append-only log of all changes; written sequentially (fast), used to reconstruct state after a crash.
123 Redo and undo Redo repeats committed changes lost from the buffer pool; undo reverses uncommitted changes that made it to disk.
124 Checkpoints and restart A checkpoint marks a safe point; recovery starts from the last checkpoint and replays the WAL forward from there.
125 Steal and no-steal Steal: dirty pages from uncommitted transactions may be written to disk (requires undo). No-steal: only committed pages are flushed (simpler).
126 Force and no-force Force: committed pages are flushed at commit time (slow). No-force: flush lazily (fast, but requires redo on crash).
127 Crash-safe commits A transaction is committed when its COMMIT record is flushed to the WAL — not when data pages hit disk.
128 Recovering unfinished work ARIES (and similar algorithms) use three passes: analysis (identify dirty pages), redo (replay history), undo (roll back losers).
129 Why recovery shapes design Recovery requirements drive WAL structure, buffer pool policies, and checkpoint frequency — they cannot be an afterthought.
