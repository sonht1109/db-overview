Part VI. Engineering Practice

Chapter 26. Choosing a Database

A practical decision framework

The previous pages in this chapter examined individual dimensions of the database selection problem: workload analysis, key questions, the build-vs-buy trade-off, managed vs self-hosted, team and cost considerations, lock-in, and when to switch. This final page assembles those dimensions into a single, actionable framework you can apply to a real decision — not a theoretical one, but the concrete choice in front of your team right now.

The Framework in Four Steps

Step 1: Write Down Your Workload

Before evaluating any database, write a one-page description of your workload. It should answer:

  • What are the five most-frequent reads and writes?
  • What are the peak volumes (requests/second) for each?
  • What is the p99 latency target for user-facing queries?
  • What is the dataset size today and in 12 months?
  • What consistency guarantee does the business require?
  • What is the schema evolution pace (stable vs weekly changes)?

If you cannot answer these questions, you are not ready to choose a database. The time spent gathering these numbers is the highest-leverage investment in the decision process.

Step 3: Score the Shortlist

After step 2 you should have two or three candidate systems. Score each on these dimensions (1–5 scale, weighted by importance to your situation):

Dimension Weight (example) Why it matters
Access pattern fit 5 Structural — cannot be worked around
Consistency guarantee 4 Business requirement — non-negotiable
Write throughput 3 Only relevant if writes dominate
Team operational expertise 4 Determines incident recovery quality
Total cost of ownership 3 Infrastructure + engineering + risk
Schema evolution ease 2 Product velocity impact
Lock-in risk 2 Long-term strategic exposure

Multiply each score by its weight, sum the columns, and compare. This is not a mechanical algorithm — it is a forcing function for making trade-offs explicit and visible to the whole team, not just the person who found the interesting new database.

Step 4: Run a Time-Bounded Proof of Concept

Before committing, validate the top candidate with your actual data shapes and query patterns. A POC that takes more than two weeks is probably too large; two weeks is enough to validate:

  • Can the system ingest data at your peak write volume?
  • Can it serve your critical queries within the latency budget?
  • Does the failure and recovery behaviour match your expectations?
  • Is the team able to set up monitoring and understand what they see?

A failed POC is a success — it saved you from a six-month migration commitment to the wrong system.

Applying the Framework: Three Scenarios

Scenario A: Early-stage startup, three engineers, building a SaaS product. Workload: standard CRUD, evolving schema, <1K users. Team expertise: web application background, limited DBA experience. Decision: PostgreSQL on a managed service (Supabase, RDS, Render). Standard SQL, managed operations, easy to hire around, scales to millions of rows before tuning is needed.

Scenario B: E-commerce platform, 50 engineers, 10M daily active users. Workload: product catalog (search-heavy), order processing (strong consistency, transactional), session management (high-volume point lookups), analytics (large aggregates, daily reports). Decision: PostgreSQL for orders and users; Redis for sessions; Elasticsearch for catalog search; Redshift or BigQuery for analytics. Each system chosen for one access pattern; PostgreSQL is the system of record.

Scenario C: IoT platform, 8 engineers, 100K devices sending sensor data every 10 seconds. Workload: 1M writes/second, queries are always time-range aggregates (average, max, percentile), data retention 90 days. Decision: TimescaleDB or InfluxDB, self-hosted on commodity hardware. The write volume and query pattern are squarely time-series; a relational database would require aggressive partitioning and would still underperform on compression and rollup queries.

The Default Rule

When in doubt — when the workload is not yet well-understood, the team is small, or the product is still finding its shape — the right answer is almost always:

PostgreSQL, managed, single instance.

It handles OLTP transactions, has excellent JSON support for semi-structured data, supports full-text search at moderate scale, can serve analytical queries on reasonable dataset sizes, and is operationally simple on a managed platform. The cases where it is genuinely the wrong choice will announce themselves clearly: either through structural performance ceilings or through query requirements it cannot express. At that point, you will have real data about your real workload and can make a precise, justified switch.

Key Takeaways

  • The framework has four steps: document the workload, filter by access pattern, score the shortlist on weighted criteria, and validate the winner with a time-bounded POC.
  • Access pattern is the single most powerful filter — it alone eliminates most candidates.
  • Make trade-offs explicit with a weighted scoring table so the whole team sees the reasoning, not just the conclusion.
  • The default is PostgreSQL on a managed service: reach for something else only when a specific, measured need makes it necessary.
