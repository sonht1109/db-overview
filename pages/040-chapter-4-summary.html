Chapter 4 Summary: What We Learned

Chapter Contents

Page Topic Takeaway in One Sentence
031 From real world to schema Start by identifying real-world entities (people, orders, products) and their relationships before designing tables.
032 Entities and relationships Entities become tables; relationships become foreign keys or join tables — this is the ER modeling approach.
033 One-to-one, one-to-many, many-to-many Cardinality determines whether you need a foreign key column (1:N) or a join table (M:N).
034 Lookup tables and enums Enums are fast but hard to change; lookup tables (reference tables with foreign keys) offer flexibility at a small join cost.
035 Derived data and computed columns Store what is factual; compute derived values (age from birthdate, total from line items) — never duplicate computations.
036 Naming tables and columns Consistent naming (singular table names, snake_case, descriptive column names) prevents years of confusion.
037 Avoiding duplication Duplication is the enemy of correctness: store each fact once, use joins to reassemble.
038 Evolving a schema safely Use expand-only migrations (add columns, never rename), backfill data, and run in stages to avoid downtime.
039 Tradeoffs in modeling Every modeling decision trades simplicity against flexibility — document your reasoning alongside your schema.
